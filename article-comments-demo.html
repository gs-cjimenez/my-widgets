Finding the Right Grief Support Group for Healing

By Sharon Jayson, AARP  ·  Published October 10, 2025  ·  6 min read

Navigating the territory of bereavement and grief can be overwhelming amid daily responsibilities. Experts say the right support group can be a lifeline — but finding the best fit takes some thoughtful reflection on your own needs and circumstances.

Whether you've lost a spouse, a parent, a child, or a close friend, the kind of community that surrounds you during recovery matters enormously. "Grief is not a problem to be solved," says Dr. Marie Hartley, a bereavement counselor with 20 years of experience. "It's a process to be witnessed — and the right group makes that process feel less lonely."

In-Person vs. Online Groups

For some, the physical presence of others who understand is irreplaceable. For others, the flexibility and anonymity of an online community lowers the barrier to opening up. Many grief professionals now recommend starting online if in-person feels too intense.

"You don't have to be ready to talk. Sometimes just reading what others share is the first step in recognizing you're not alone in how you feel."

— Dr. Marie Hartley, Bereavement Counselor

The AARP Grief & Loss community forum has become a trusted gathering place for members navigating these experiences. Members share resources, personal stories, and practical advice in a moderated, compassionate environment.

Questions to Ask Before Joining

Before committing to a group, consider: Is it facilitated by a licensed counselor or a peer volunteer? How often does it meet? Is it specific to your type of loss? A mismatch in group focus can sometimes deepen isolation rather than relieve it.

National organizations like GriefShare, the Compassionate Friends, and AARP's own online community offer structured programs with trained facilitators — a good starting point for anyone uncertain where to begin.
